Week in Review — Premium Desk

The week of 27 May 2026 finds markets navigating the most complex macro environment since 2022: a fully embedded stagflation regime in the United States, a contracting eurozone, and a geopolitical wildfire in the Middle East that refuses to be contained. The US-Iran war — now entering its third month — remains the structural driver across every asset class. The Strait of Hormuz partial blockade has locked Brent crude above $95/bbl, and that energy premium is transmitting directly into inflation prints that the Federal Reserve cannot ignore.

The data this month has been unambiguous. US PCE in Q1 2026 came in at 4.5% against a prior of 2.9% — a shock print that, combined with the GDP advance estimate missing at 2.0% vs 2.3% forecast, delivers the textbook stagflation signal: growth decelerating while inflation reaccelerates. The May 12 CPI print confirmed the trend — 3.8% vs 3.6% expected — pushing yields higher and sending Gold from $4,490 back to the $4,520–$4,580 supply zone. The Dollar has stabilised above 99 on DXY, the FOMC Minutes released this month leaned hawkish with a majority flagging hikes as appropriate if inflation persists, and new Fed Chair Kevin Warsh — sworn in May 22 — is being watched closely for any policy shift signal.

On the eurozone side, the Composite PMI collapsed to 47.5 in the May flash reading — the sharpest contraction since October 2023 — dragging EUR/USD to a six-week low near 1.1580 before partial recovery. Despite the PMI miss, Euro Area CPI held firm at 3.1% vs 2.9% expected, locking the ECB into its own stagflation corner. A June ECB hike of 25bp to 2.25% is now near-certain, per ING and Conference Board. The key risk event for the remainder of this week is the US GDP Second Estimate due 28 May — consensus holds at 2.0% but a downside miss could reignite growth-scare positioning. US PCE Price Index follows shortly after. Both prints will determine whether the Fed is forced into a 2026 hike cycle or holds through year-end.
