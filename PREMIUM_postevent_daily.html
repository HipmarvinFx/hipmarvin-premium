Week in Review — What Drove the Market · 20 May 2026

The week of 18 May opened with a macro shock that initially threatened the USD bull thesis: Moody's stripped the United States of its final Aaa credit rating on Friday 16 May, citing the $36tn debt load and rising interest burden. Monday's reaction was brief — EUR/USD jumped toward 1.1640, DXY dipped toward 99.00 — before the market remembered that a credit downgrade in an inflationary environment paradoxically steepens the yield curve rather than weakening the Dollar. By Tuesday, USD was fully bid again. DXY reclaimed 99.40 and held it through Wednesday.

The second major event of the day: UK CPI came in softer than expected this morning — below consensus — removing the last justification for GBP strength. GBP/USD dropped back toward 1.3400 and is currently pressing an 8-day low. The BoE next meets on 18 June; today's soft CPI print increases the probability of a cut at that meeting. EUR/GBP is worth watching — both currencies are softening but at different rates.

Gold is at its lowest level since 30 March. XAU/USD has broken through $4,480 support on an intraday basis, with the pair trading near $4,472. Real yields are driving this move: as US 10-year yields remain elevated near recent highs and the Fed signals no imminent cuts, the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding gold increases. The 186K+ net long position from institutional traders is being steadily liquidated — that is the waterfall setup described in last week's analysis, now in motion.

Elijah's Kani Premium Take — 20 May 2026
What today's FOMC Minutes confirm is not just a hawkish Fed — it confirms a structural shift in how the committee itself frames risk. When a majority of members internally debate whether to remove the easing bias — in what was meant to be a routine hold meeting — that is a sea change. The market's muted reaction to the minutes is not a sign of indifference. It is a sign that the hawkish repricing is already deeply embedded in positioning. CME FedWatch puts a December 2026 rate hike at 40.1% probability. That is not a fringe view — that is the market's base case. What changes the narrative from here: a genuine peace deal on Iran (oil down, inflation down, cuts back on the table) or a labour market break (unemployment spikes, Fed forced to pivot). Neither looks imminent. Until then, USD bulls hold the floor. Trade the regime, not the noise.
The "Why" — Fundamentals Decoded · Chain Reaction
Step 1 · Iran War → Oil Stays High
 The Strait of Hormuz closure continues to restrict ~20% of global oil and LNG shipments. Trump paused a strike plan Tuesday after Gulf allies intervened, but the structural supply disruption remains. Crude prices are still structurally elevated. Oil up = energy inflation up = CPI stays hot.
Step 2 · Hot CPI → Fed Stays on Hold (or Hikes) April CPI: 3.7% (prior 3.3%). PPI also accelerated at its fastest pace since 2022. The Fed's 2% target is nowhere in sight. Today's minutes confirm internal debate has shifted from "when to cut" to "should we hike." The CME FedWatch December 2026 hike probability is now 40.1%.
Step 3 · Higher Rates → Dollar Attracts Capital US rates at 3.50–3.75% vs ECB near 2.0%, BoE at 3.75%, BoJ near zero. The yield gap makes dollar-denominated assets more attractive to global investors. Capital flows into USD. DXY holds above 99.40 and eyes 100.00.
Step 4 · Bond Vigilantes + Moody's Downgrade → Yields Rise Further The Moody's downgrade accelerated the long-end yield sell-off. US 10-year yields are pressing multi-year highs. Rising real yields are the primary force squeezing Gold. This is the paradox of the downgrade: worse credit = higher yields = stronger Dollar in the short term.
Step 5 · Dollar Up → Gold Down, EUR/GBP Down, AUD/USD Down Gold trades inversely to real yields and the Dollar — both headwinds are live. EUR/USD is at an 8-day low. AUD/USD has lost nearly 2% since last week as China demand fears compound USD strength. The carry trade (borrow JPY, buy USD assets) is being rebuilt — USD/JPY approaches 159.20, nearing the January high at 159.45.
⚠ Regime Alert — Easing Bias Being Dismantled
The April FOMC minutes represent the first formal institutional record of the Fed's internal pivot from cuts to potential hikes. Three voting members dissented against the easing bias — and a fourth indicated she would have joined them. This is not fringe hawkishness. This is a majority realignment. Combined with Kevin Warsh now taking the chair, the new Fed is structurally more hawkish than at any point since 2022. Any trade built on a 2026 rate-cut narrative is now operating against the grain of the institution itself. Adjust all EUR/USD, Gold, and AUD/USD bullish positions accordingly. The easing bias is not gone yet — but today's minutes confirm it is on borrowed time.
