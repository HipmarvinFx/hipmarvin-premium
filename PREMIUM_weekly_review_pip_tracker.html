R:R Framework — How Elijah Sizes Risk
Standard rule: 1% account risk per trade. On high-conviction weeks (macro + COT + structure all aligned), I scale to 1.5%. I never exceed 1.5% on a single position, regardless of conviction. On weeks like this — where the macro narrative is clear and the COT data is confirming — the goal is to be in every aligned setup at full size. Six trades at 1–1.5% risk each = maximum 7.5% portfolio risk across the week. That is a controlled, professional exposure. If all six had stopped out simultaneously (near impossible, given different pairs), the account would be down 7.5% — an uncomfortable but survivable drawdown. Managing the portfolio ceiling, not just individual trades, is what professional risk management looks like.

How to Use COT Data in Your Own Trading
COT data is released every Friday for positions as of Tuesday. There is always a lag. Don't use COT to enter trades — use it to filter direction. If COT says commercials are net long JPY and your setup is short JPY, that is a red flag — reduce size or skip it. If COT aligns with your setup direction, size up. The GBP/USD loss this week is a perfect example: COT was showing GBP buying. The technical setup was a short. Those two signals were in conflict. A smaller size or a pass would have been the disciplined call. Going forward: if technical and COT conflict, default to 0.5R or pass entirely.
Five Lessons from This Week
1
Process
Macro Context Overrides Technical Structure
The GBP/USD short was technically valid — but the macro environment made any USD-long a low-probability trade. Before entering any setup, ask: does this trade align with the dominant macro theme this week? If the answer is no, reduce size or skip the trade entirely.
2
From a Win
The News is the Exit, Not the Entry
USD/JPY and EUR/USD were both positioned before the CPI release — based on COT data and institutional flow. By the time CPI printed, the trade was already 60% done. Retail traders entered on the news. Smart money was already taking profit.
3
From a Loss
A Planned Loss is Not a Failed Trade
The GBP/USD loss of −46 pips was stopped out at the pre-defined level. The stop held. The trade closed cleanly. A loss with a plan is not a failure — it is the cost of doing business. Never move your stop to avoid a loss. Take it, review it, learn from it.
4
Process
Partial Profits Protect Gains When Conditions Shift
XAU/USD started reversing on Thursday when DXY bounced. Rather than hold and hope, the half position was closed at +104 pips and the remainder moved to breakeven. Condition management mid-trade is a skill, not a weakness. Know when the thesis is weakening — and act.
5
From a Win
When COT and Chart Align, Size Up Confidently
USD/JPY and EUR/USD were both COT-confirmed and technically clean. That rare alignment justified 1.5R sizing on both. The best trades are the ones where everything agrees. When macro, COT and chart all point the same direction — that is when you swing harder.

How to Use This Week's Numbers
This week's average stop was 42 pips across the 6 trades. At 1% risk on a $10,000 account, that means a position size of approximately 0.24 lots per trade. At 1.5% (high-conviction trades like USD/JPY and EUR/USD this week): 0.36 lots. Always size to the stop — never size to the target. The number of pips you plan to make is irrelevant to your position size. The only number that matters is: how many pips to the stop?
Elijah's Position Sizing Rules
Rule 1: Never risk more than 1.5% on any single trade. Rule 2: Never have more than 5% total portfolio risk open at any one time across all positions. Rule 3: On a losing streak of 3 or more consecutive losses, cut position size to 0.5R until a win re-establishes confidence. Rule 4: Winning streaks are not a reason to size up — they are a reason to stay disciplined. Rule 5: Your position size should be boring. If you feel anything when placing a trade — excitement, fear, hope — the size is wrong. Size down until you feel nothing. That is the professional standard.
