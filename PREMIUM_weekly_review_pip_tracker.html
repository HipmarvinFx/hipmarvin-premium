Week in Review

The week ending 23 May 2026 was a conviction-led week defined by one dominant event: the hawkish FOMC Minutes on Wednesday. Five setups were flagged. Respecting the partial exit preserved the week's second-best gain and avoided giving it back in the chaotic Friday close.

R:R Framework — How Elijah Sizes Risk
Standard rule: 1% account risk per trade. On high-conviction weeks (macro + COT + structure all aligned), I scale to 1.5%. I never exceed 1.5% on a single position, regardless of conviction. This week, USD/JPY and EUR/USD both got 1.5R sizing — COT data confirmed USD institutional accumulation and the hawkish FOMC setup was telegraphed by the data well in advance. GBP/USD received a standard 1R because it was a post-event trade with acknowledged binary risk. Sizing discipline around known event risk is not a luxury — it is what keeps one binary stop-out from derailing a winning week. Five trades at 1–1.5% risk = 5.5% total portfolio exposure max. Even if all five had stopped simultaneously, the account survives intact to trade next week. That is the only goal of risk management.

How to Use COT Data in Your Own Trading
COT data is released every Friday for positions as of Tuesday. There is always a lag. Don't use COT to enter trades — use it to filter direction. If COT says specs are building GBP shorts at a 15-week extreme, and your setup is also a GBP short, that tells you two things: (1) the primary trend is confirmed, and (2) the risk of a violent squeeze is elevated because everyone is already on the same side. The GBP/USD stop-out this week is the perfect illustration — the COT was screaming "extreme short" going into the BoE. An extreme short positioning into a binary central bank event is a warning, not a green light. Going forward: when COT is at an extreme and a binary event is in the same week, reduce position size to 0.5R and widen the stop to account for the potential squeeze move. Or skip entirely.

Process

The portfolio ceiling was reduced to 2 concurrent positions this week — down from a normal maximum of 3 — because FOMC and BoE were both in the same 72-hour window. If a third position had been open when the BoE hawkish dissent triggered, there would have been simultaneous exposure across GBP, Gold and another pair. That constraint is not conservative — it is professional risk architecture. Reduce exposure before known volatility events, not after.

From a Win
Exit AUD/USD Before the Next Event Risk, Not After
The AUD/USD short hit TP1 at 0.7180 Wednesday — before the BoE on Thursday. The position was fully closed at target before the next known risk event. This sequence — take profit before the next binary event, not through it — is the correct professional approach. Had the AUD trade still been open into the BoE, any GBP-positive reaction could have spilled into a brief AUD short squeeze, threatening the already-secured gain.

How to Use This Week's Numbers
This week's average stop was 65 pips across the 5 trades. At 1% risk on a $10,000 account, that means a position size of approximately 0.15 lots per trade. At 1.5% (high-conviction trades like USD/JPY and EUR/USD this week): 0.23 lots. Always size to the stop — never size to the target. The number of pips you plan to make is irrelevant to your position size. The only number that matters is: how many pips to the stop?
Elijah's Position Sizing Rules
Rule 1: Never risk more than 1.5% on any single trade. Rule 2: Never have more than 5% total portfolio risk open at any one time across all positions. Rule 3: On a losing streak of 3 or more consecutive losses, cut position size to 0.5R until a win re-establishes confidence. Rule 4: Winning streaks are not a reason to size up — they are a reason to stay disciplined. Rule 5: Your position size should be boring. If you feel anything when placing a trade — excitement, fear, hope — the size is wrong. Size down until you feel nothing. That is the professional standard.
